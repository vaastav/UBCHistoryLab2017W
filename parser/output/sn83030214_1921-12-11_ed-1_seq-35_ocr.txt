Medium Grade Wool In Better Demand In Boston Market Special Dispatch to The Tribune BOSTON, Dec. 10.?Strong demand for medium grades of wool has been a prominent feature of the woo] mar? ket for the last week. The call has ranged from wools of a quarter-blood grade to those of half-blood quality, particularly the leatetr. Like fine wools, however, half-blood grades are scarce, and so the trade is obliged to resort to the three-eighths-blood wools to a great extent. Owing also to the scarcity of half-bloods, fair-sized lots changed hands three and four times in one day. Thi3 exceeds the frequency of transactions in the same lots of wool reported the previous week. One of the sales of medium grades of wool listed of 900 bales of Punta Arenas, a South American wool, totaling ap? proximately 900,000 pounds. The quarter-blood and three-eighths blood wools are understood to have been bought for knitting and weaving purposes. In spite of the activity in the raw wol market, some of which is speculation, there is a vast difference in the amount of operation among the mills. A well known wool dealer re? porte: "You go to one spinner and he say* that there is nothing to do and he ;s ready to close down. Then you go to another who is spinning the same kind of yarn and he says that he is booked ahead for two or three months." With all these irregularities, however, the fact remains that spinners have been willing and even anxious to cover on their forward requirements in tops, the long, combed out fibres, at con? siderably advanced prices. Bullish conditions in the wool mar? ket have led to overtures for the pur? chase of wool on the sheep's back in the West. It is reported that 20 cents a pound has been offered for certain clips in Utah, but that, the wool inter- j there refuse to consider that figure. The conservative element in the trade does not encourage such "movement, but it is stated, on tre other hand, that actual buying of wool before it is ?horn may begin at any time. Much of this scramble for wool has arisen from the assumption that the emergency tariff will remain in force for a protracted period. The trade, however, is hoping that the por? tion of President Harding's message referring to the tariff will stimulate Action on the part of Congress, so that a definite, reasonable rate of duty on raw wool will be established. This wouid clarify the situation, tend toward an equitable balance on wool, tops, yarn and pieces, allow the manufac? turers to formulate their plans and prevent the growers from profiteering.
